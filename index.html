Syllabus — with primers

Each topic has a primer you can read right here, plus where to go deeper. Open a stage, open a topic, and tick it off once you could explain it to someone else.
Stage 1 · Foundation (weeks 1–6)
Push or fold at 15–20bb
Below about 20bb, open-raising and then folding to a shove bleeds chips, so your main weapon becomes the jam: it wins the blinds and antes uncontested or gets your stack in with fold equity plus hand equity. The core rules: jam wider as fewer players remain behind you, wider with antes in play, wider in later position, and tighter when the players behind call too loosely. Blind vs blind at 10bb, equilibrium has the SB jamming about 58% of hands — far wider than feels natural, which is exactly why drilling matters. Any ace, any pair, and most suited kings are jams at short depth.

Drill: open the Solver’s Nash tool, solve 8, 12, 16 and 20bb, and guess each hand before hovering. Then use Jam EV for non-blind seats.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: Playing Under 10bb (Part 1) · How Stack Sizes Change Your Range · HoldemResources calculator (cross-check)
Bubble play
On the bubble, chips you lose hurt more than chips you win help, and the effect is biggest for medium stacks. The playbook: big stack — attack relentlessly, especially medium stacks, because they cannot call you without risking the min-cash they are one elimination from locking up. Medium stack — avoid confrontation with anyone who covers you; steal from shorter stacks instead. Short stack — if micro, folding into the money is often right; if you still have fold equity, pick one clean jam spot rather than blinding out. Calling ranges tighten dramatically on the bubble; jamming ranges tighten far less. That asymmetry is where the money is.
Go deeper: PokerNews: Abusing the Bubble · GTO Wizard: Money Bubble vs Final Table Bubble
Basic final table ICM
Chip value is not linear: doubling your chips less than doubles your prize equity, because part of every stack’s value is the chance everyone else busts first. Practical consequences: calls need meaningfully more equity than chip math says (marginal calls become folds), while jams stay close to normal; and with a short stack, folding through a pay jump is often worth more than a coin flip.

Drill: in the ICM tab, enter a final table you played. Compare your $EV before a big call, after winning it, and after losing it — the asymmetry is the whole lesson.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: When Does ICM Become Significant? · How Payout Structures Impact ICM
Stage 2 · Intermediate depth (weeks 7–16)
Advanced ICM: laddering & pressure
The working concept is the bubble factor: how much more a lost chip costs you than a won chip gains you. At a normal final table it pushes your required calling equity from 50% up to 55–60%+ against a shove, and higher still when a micro stack is about to bust. Applications: ladder patiently when someone is nearly out; as the big stack, jam into the medium stacks who suffer the worst bubble factors; never go to war with the second-biggest stack without a premium. Model real spots in ICMIZER once you finish this stage’s reading.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: What Is the Bubble Factor? · Playing Under 10bb (Part 2: ICM) · the book Kill Everyone (Nelson, Heston & Streib) — origin of the bubble factor
Exploitative live reads
Live low and mid stakes fields share reliable population tendencies: limps mean weak-passive holdings, big river bets are almost always value (population under-bluffs rivers badly), snap-calls are draws or medium pairs, and long tanks that end in a raise are monsters. So: bluff-catch far less on rivers, isolate limpers relentlessly, and give big turn/river aggression from passive players immense credit. Keep a book on your regulars — the Notes column in your session log is for exactly this. Reads beat equilibrium in soft fields; that is the whole argument for playing live.
Go deeper: PokerNews: Live Reads & Tells · Upswing: Tactics to Crush Live Poker · PokerCoaching: 3 Common Live Exploits
Multiway pots
Every extra player in the pot slashes your equity and your fold equity at once. Adjustments: c-bet far less often (someone usually connected), value-bet thinner hands less and strong hands harder, nearly stop bluffing rivers multiway, and upgrade draws — suited and connected hands realize equity better multiway than offsuit high cards do. Top pair weak kicker, a fine hand heads-up, becomes a check-caller at best three ways.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: 10 Tips for Multiway Pots · Monkey in the Middle: 3-Way Heuristics

Solver-backed review of your own hands

Postflop hands go into GTO Wizard: replicate the spot, commit to what you think the solution is, then check. Log the lesson either way. ICMIZER simulations
Rebuild final table and bubble spots you actually played with real payouts and stacks. Your goal is calibration: before each sim, write down how much you think ICM shifts the chip EV answer, then measure your miss. After twenty of these your in-game ICM instinct is a real asset.

Opponent modeling
Build range profiles for your regular pool: who jams too wide, who never bluff-shoves, who calls too loose. Then stop studying pure equilibrium against them — feed your read into the Jam EV tool’s calling-range input and solve against the player in front of you. Equilibrium is the baseline; deviation from it against known opponents is the profit.

Stage 4 · Postflop & deep stacks (weeks 17–28)
Deep-stack preflop: opens, 3-bets, blind defense
The early levels are the opposite game to the endgame you drilled first: 40–100bb deep, position dominates and postflop skill gets paid. Open small (2.2–2.5x) so mistakes are cheap and you can fold to jams; open tighter early position, much wider on the button. From the BB you defend very wide against small opens — the price is too good to fold most suited and connected hands — but remember you'll realize that equity badly out of position, so defend by calling, not by bloating pots. 3-bet a linear range (best hands, no fancy bluffs) against weak openers in soft fields, and avoid flat-calling out of position deep: it's the highest-leak habit in live poker.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: Defending the Blinds in MTTs · Small Blind, Big Problems (BvB)
C-betting & board textures
Stop asking “did I hit?” and start asking “whose range does this flop favor?” High, dry boards (A72 rainbow) smash the preflop raiser: c-bet often and small. Low, connected boards (765 two-tone) belong to the caller: check far more, and size up when you do bet. Out of position the solver checks far more than feels natural — as an early-position raiser, equilibrium c-bets under 30% of the time. Small bets on static boards, big bets on dynamic ones, and every bet should have a reason: fold out equity, build a pot for value, or deny a free card.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: OOP C-Betting in MTTs · IP C-Betting Heuristics · C-Betting As the OOP Raiser
Hand reading: narrowing ranges street by street
Never put someone on “a hand” — put them on a range at the moment they enter the pot, then subtract combinations with every action. A live rec who limp-calls has a range; when he check-raises the turn on a wet board, cross off everything that doesn't make sense and what remains is your answer. The key concept is the capped range: a player whose actions rule out the strongest holdings can't defend against big pressure — find caps, then attack them. Practice: pick one opponent per session and narrate their range street by street in your head, whether you're in the hand or not.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: Capped Ranges & Bluffing Imbalances · the book Modern Poker Theory (Acevedo) — range-construction chapters
Stage 5 · Endgame & formats (months 7–12)
Short-handed & heads-up endgame
Final tables compress from 9 players to 3 to heads-up, and every elimination should widen your ranges — hands that were folds 9-handed become opens 5-handed and premiums 3-handed. Most players adjust too slowly and bleed away the blinds at the sharpest pay jumps. Heads-up at short stacks is near any-two-cards territory for the small blind: you already have the tool for this — the Nash tab solves any stack depth, and the equilibrium there IS heads-up endgame strategy. Solve 5, 8, 12 and 15bb heads-up and know those shapes cold before your next final table.
Go deeper: HoldemResources HU calculator (cross-check)
Deal-making: ICM chops
Three deal methods exist and they are not equal. Even chop: only fair with even stacks. Chip chop: pays by chip share — systematically overpays the big stack, and big stacks will push for it. ICM chop: weights every possible finishing order — the fair baseline. The rule: never negotiate blind. Run the numbers in this app's ICM tab before you say a word, know your ICM figure, and treat it as your floor if you believe you're better than the field — skill earns a premium above ICM, never a discount below it. Deals are won by the player who arrives knowing the numbers.
Go deeper: PokerNews: ICM and Chip Chop Explained · GTO Wizard: How To Negotiate FT Deals · The 4 Big Questions of Deal-Making
PKO & bounty adjustments
In bounty tournaments part of the prize pool sits on each player's head, and that money changes everything. The core rule: when you cover an opponent, their bounty adds directly to your calling equity — call jams meaningfully wider than normal maths says. When you're the covered one, expect to get called wider, so jam tighter for value and bluff-jam less. Early in a PKO, bounties are large relative to stacks and the correct game is startlingly loose; as stacks deepen relative to remaining bounties, play reverts toward normal. If you play PKOs regularly, this is a separate discipline — study it separately.
Go deeper: GTO Wizard: How Does ICM Impact PKO Strategy? · The Theory of Bounty Tournaments (Part 2)
Stage 6 · The mental game (ongoing)
Tilt: the ROI killer
Tilt is anger-driven decision error, and it costs skilled live players more than any strategic leak. The crucial insight from Tendler's work: anger is a symptom — the disease is an underlying belief, usually an entitlement to fairness (“I played it perfectly, I deserved to win”) or ownership of results you don't control. Map your personal tilt profile: what specifically sets you off, what it looks like in your play (calling wider? forcing bluffs?), and your earliest warning sign. Then set mechanical defenses: a stop-loss, a walk after every big lost pot, and a rule that big decisions get five breaths. Log your emotional state in session notes — the pattern will teach you.
Go deeper: PokerNews: Tilt — A Phenomenal ROI Killer · the book The Mental Game of Poker (Jared Tendler) — the standard text
Variance & the long run
Winning MTT regs routinely experience downswings of 20–50+ buy-ins; if that sentence surprises you, you are not emotionally prepared for this game. Judge yourself on decisions, never on outcomes — a correct call that loses is a win, a bad jam that gets there is a loss. This is why the app grades your decisions against equilibrium rather than showing you results, why 100 tournaments proves nothing about your win rate, and why the bankroll gates stay strict. Set process goals (sessions reviewed, drills done, topics studied) — the results goals will take care of themselves or they won't, and neither is fully in your hands.
Go deeper: Tendler: In Defense of Results-Oriented Goals · your own dashboard's honesty check, reread after every downswing
Session routine & game selection
Game selection is the biggest edge in live poker — a mediocre player in a soft lineup out-earns an expert in a tough one, every time. Choose venues and days where the recreational money plays, and if the seat to your right has the table's money, you're in the right chair. Then protect your edge with routine: five minutes before playing, review your last lesson and clear your flashcards; during play, take a real break every 90 minutes and keep notes on regulars; after play, log the session while it's fresh and mark 3–5 hands for review. The routine is what separates players who improve from players who just accumulate hours.
